Section 01

High Level Concept Design

1.1 Working Title

The game's title is Bound Memories. The title has 2 meanings; "Bound" refers to the player's situation - they are physically bound/stuck in a prison that they have sealed themselves into - and tied to a past that they cannot escape. "Memories" is the entire goal of the game: chasing them, uncovering them, and deciding whether they are worth keeping. The title says what the game is about in two words.

1.2 Concept Statement

"You wake inside an underground prison, sealed there by your past self, with no memory as to why. Following the only path available to you, you uncover what you did - and face a choice: are your memories really worth keeping?"

1.3 Genre

Bound Memories combines 2 genres, each adding to the last without them conflicting:

Puzzle-Adventure

The game is exploration driven. The player moves through a large underground citadel called Destruction's Gaze, discovering areas, unlocking items, and solving environmental puzzles. The puzzles are driven by the mirror world - a parallel dimension accessible through... mirrors (and other reflective surfaces) - where physics and nature behave differently from the real world.

Rhythm Combat

Combat is built around listening to the music. Attacking on beat with an instrument builds towards activating its elemental effect. The player must listen actively and react to what is currently playing.

1.4 Target Audience

Primary Audience

  • Ages 12+
  • Players who like indie games (such as) - Undertale, Hollow Knight, Celeste
  • Players who enjoy music as a gameplay element, like in rhythm games

Rated T for Teen - difficult mechanics, fantasy violence, scary content (imprisonment, guilt, amnesia) and mild horror (floating eyes and a pursuing creature in the mirror realm)

1.5 Unique Selling Points

Music Based Combat

Most other games use music as atmosphere. In Bound Memories, the music is an actual functional part of the combat. Each instrument has an associated element. (see 3.2, System 2) Five consecutive, on beat attacks timed to a specific instrument (to make sure which instrument you are attacking with) activates that instrument's effect. Harmony windows open when multiple instruments' attacks sync up, combining each instrument's element and making each attack more powerful.

A Parallel World With Its Own Physics

The mirror world (accessible through reflective surfaces) is similar to the real world, but distorted and blue/purple. Many things are different there such as:

  • The gravity
  • The structures
  • The mechanics

The longer a player stays there, the worse it gets.

MinutesWhat happens
1Whispers start, your shadow glitches, and the music distorts slightly.
2Eyes start to surround you, and the distortions grow. An unsettling, distant growling begins…
2.75 (2 mins 45 secs)The eyes start shooting beams of purple matter at you. It doesn't do much damage, but there are lots of them, and it makes noise…
3 minsA hostile creature charges at the player, and it can follow the player back into the real world. It is really dangerous..

Diegetic UI

There are barely any traditional menus or interfaces. It is all within the world. Most UI elements live inside of a book which the player carries, written by their past self (when they had their memories). It has instructions, logs/ diary entries, controls/keybinds, the exit button, etc

Vocal Power Indicators

Every character's "voice" is short musical tones (I like to call them beepy boops) which play alongside text (kinda like Undertale, which now that I think about it heavily inspired the game, subconsciously). However, lower pitch indicates more power, and everything follows this rule. The player has the lowest tone/pitch of anything, which adds to the mystery (if the player figures it out).

Section 02

Product Design

2.1 Player Experience and Game POV

Who Is The Player?

The player controls an amnesiac sorcerer - a magic user who can access parallel dimensions. They wake with no memories or any recollection of their past self. A book sits on the other side of the cave, no name or anything. Everything the player learns comes from that book. It seems to be enchanted, as it can move slightly on its own, so that it can direct the player towards the right direction.

The character is always cloaked, and their face is never visible. Before losing their memories, the character left one rule in the book: "NEVER show your face under ANY circumstances."

What Is The Setting?

The game takes place entirely underground. The primary location is the citadel - a vast, gothic fortress which rises high above the streets of the city which is called the citadel's foot. It is all run by the boss of a mafia called the mafia boss (wow I don't have an imagination), whose eye symbol is carved everywhere.

The citadel is run by "evolved" humans (people who have wings on their heads), goblins, and the undead. They run shops, trade, and can maybe be friends with you.

What Fantasy Does The Game Grant?

You can be the most powerful entity in the room, but you don't know who you are or what you are doing there. The player can access a parallel dimension, and unleash elemental attacks, yet they don't even know their own name. The game is about the choice between power and identity, or peace and oblivion.

Target Emotions

  • Growing dread — the mirror world grows more hostile and distorted over time; the eyes in the reflection get closer and clearer throughout the game
  • Curiosity — the mystery of what the character did, the strange world around them, lore that never gets fully explained
  • Melancholy — at the end, neither ending feels right, and you always lose out, even if it was the "better" option
  • Occasional comedy — some characters are just designed to be pea-brained and stupid, to make the player laugh, usually before a dangerous moment so their spirits are lifted

2.2 Visual and Audio Style

Visual Style

The game is 2D pixel art, but from an isometric perspective if possible. Depth sorting could be handled by assigning depth values based on vertical position.

The real world uses warmer, dim cave tones (such as browns, dark greens, oranges). The mirror world is similar to the real world, but the main difference is the cool tones (like cold blue, purples, pinks).

Audio Style

Strings are the most used, like violin, and cello. Piano will also be a main instrument used. Other instruments can be introduced depending on the mood, like breakcore drums for cool bossfights, or electric guitar too. The music in the game, but mostly the bossfights, will have modular sections for the song. There may be an A section, a (slightly different to A) B section, a more intense C section, and like a calmer/dying out D section, for variety and repeatability.

Vocal Textures

Every character communicates through short notes playing alongside textboxes, similar to Undertale. However, there is one twist; The lower the note, the more powerful the entity is. The player has the lowest vocal tone in the entire game.

2.3 Game World Fiction

The world is 100% underground. The citadel is most of the game. it is a vast gothic fortress which stretches to the ceiling, with multiple towers disappearing behind stalactites. The Mafia Boss' eye symbol is engraved everywhere.

Behind every mirrored surface is the mirror world, and the player can access it whenever they see a mirror, unless it is (fully) indoors. ONLY the player is able to access the mirror world. no other people can.

The player wakes up with no memory. The opening cutscene shows them walking towards a cave, with a heavy bank style door wide open. They pause for a second, look around one last time, and walk in, closing the door behind them. The whole game is spent finding out why.

2.4 Monetization

The game can be free to play for a short demo, like the introduction, but it cuts off just before the citadel. it costs £1.75 and you can also buy cosmetics to further support me/the dev team.

2.5 Platform, Technology, and Scope

  • Platforms: PC (Windows, Linux, Mac), Mobile (PC is released first, and then mobile is released later as all of the inputs are different)
  • Engine: GameMaker
  • Team Size: 1-4 people (depending on if the other people in the group like the game enough to continue)

Major Risks

  • The modular music may be difficult to execute.
  • The whole game will need lots of optimisation
  • The rhythm beat detection may be pretty difficult to nail
Section 03

Detailed & Game Systems Design

3.1 Core Loops

Combat Loop

Encounter enemy → listen to music → identify the instrument you want → hit 5 on beat attacks to that instrument → elemental effect activates → watch for harmony windows → move on.

Exploration Loop

Move through Citadel → find puzzles → enter mirror world → solve gravity-distortion puzzle or find lore → unlock new area → accumulate book entries → deepen understanding of central mystery.

3.2 Objectives and Progression

Opening and Tutorial

The player wakes with no memory. A book they find contains a tutorial written by their past self, which they have the choice to skip (if they are speedrunning the game)

Short Term Goals (~First Hour)

  • Learn basic movement and combat
  • Discover and enter the mirror world for the first time
  • Emerge from a tunnel into the full reveal of the Citadel - one of the game's most beautiful moments

Medium-Term Goals (~Hours 1–3)

  • Explore the Citadel's multiple wings and areas
  • Try to clear your debt to the Mafia
  • Find and complete optional rat side quests
  • Discover, and decide, what to do with the mysterious pink crystal
  • Accumulate lore entries revealing the player's past

Long-Term Goals (~Hours 3–5+)

  • Reach the boss arena
  • Defeat the boss
  • Make the ending choice

System 1: The Book/Diary

There are very few, if any, normal menus. Most of what the player needs will be inside the book.

  • Save slots — Journal logs which say the date, time, and location.
  • Stats page — Four stats recorded as numbers, basically a skill tree.
  • Bestiary — Entries which describe the various creatures which you may see. The quality of the creature's sketch shows how dangerous/common it is. The calmer and more detailed a sketch, the more easily the player could see it. Some creatures may not have entries, as the past player hadn't encountered it.
  • Diary entries — The past player had enchanted the book so certain pages can only be opened when you get to certain places in the game. For example, when you get to the citadel, it gives you a tip "Don't interact with anyone related to the eye"
  • Mirror world pages — Some pages are only visible in the mirror world, and are blank in the real world.
  • Tutorial — It can be skipped, but the book can use sketches and animated sketches to teach you the mechanics, and then enemies can conveniently appear for you to practice on.

Examples of the book helping the player:

"Try... Just try to make the right choice"
"Don't carry what doesn't belong to you into places of power."
"NEVER show your face under ANY circumstances."

System 2: (Rhythm) Combat

Combat is based on the background music. Each instrument has a different effect, which are listed below. Each effect can deal more or less damage to specific types of enemies.

The Sword as Diegetic UI

Because the sword is made from musicians' souls, it responds directly to music — eliminating the need for any combat HUD. Everything the player needs to know is communicated through the blade:

  • Beat window glow: The blade brightens as the beat approaches and dims between hits — the glow intensity is the beat indicator.
  • Instrument colour: The blade's colour shifts to match whichever instrument is currently dominant in the music. Purple for bass, blue for electric guitar, pale blue-white for violin high register, deep blue for violin low register, brown-gold for cello, white for drums. The player learns to read the colour without thinking about it.
  • Hit feedback: A correct on-beat hit produces a brilliant flare along the full length of the blade. A miss makes it flicker and briefly darken — a visual wince.
  • Soul wisps: Small particles of light orbit the blade at all times. Their orbit speed increases as the beat approaches and slows just after — a secondary beat indicator for players who find colour harder to read. On a successful hit they briefly scatter outward then return. The number of wisps currently orbiting shows the current hit combo — they accumulate with each consecutive hit and scatter on a miss.
  • Harmony: When a harmony window opens, all the wisps briefly align into a single orbit pattern and the blade cycles through all active instrument colours rapidly.
  • Health: Shown through the player character's cloak — more tattered and faded as damage is taken.

images/combat/sword_ambient.pngSoul-forged blade, ambient glow state

Hit Precision Tiers

The hit window is approximately 4 ticks (200ms) by default. Hitting within this window always registers, but how close to the exact beat determines a damage bonus:

  • Within 300ms: Fine — base damage
  • Within 200ms: Good — 5% bonus
  • Within 100ms: Great — 10% bonus
  • Within 50ms: Excellent — 15% bonus

The sword's flare on a hit reflects the precision tier. No numbers appear on screen. The player reads the sword.

Any enemy hit by this will take damage over time. It works best when combined with other elements.

Does 1.5x more damage to ghost type enemies, but less damage to normal enemies. Attacks with this element can pass through solid objects.

Bright and piercing. Strong against dark enemies, weak against everything else. Pairs naturally with the organ — both are traditionally church instruments.
🎵

Small and supportive. Calms enemies slightly — reducing their movement speed and attack frequency. Does nothing loud or dramatic. Makes other things easier.
🎹
Piano
Atmospheric — No Combat Element
Present in the music as atmosphere and emotional texture. Cannot be built toward for elemental effects. Its role is entirely sonic — shaping the feel of an area or moment without providing a combat tool.

System 3: Harmony

Sometimes, multiple instruments will sync up, so there will be harmonies. These do more damage and use different elements based on the instruments which make up those harmonies.

  • 2 instruments: Small coloured beam attack
  • 3 instruments: Medium coloured beam attack
  • 4+ instruments: A massive, rainbow beam covers the enemy and when it fades, the enemy is gone. (unless it is a boss fight, then it just does a sizeable chunk of damage)

Each instrument's element has its own colour, so it is easy to tell what elements are being used in the harmony.

System 4: The Mirror World

A parallel dimension to the real world, accessible through reflective surfaces. It is similar to the real world, but certain ruins may be intact in the mirror world which aren't in reality, or certain artefacts are only available in the mirror world.

Access Rules

  • Thin boundary areas — Ancient ruins, caves, temples. Usually in places made of mirrored stone.
  • Thick boundary areas — Ordinary places such as the houses and taverns. The mirror world cannot reach inside of them.

What Happens Inside The Mirror World

  • 0–2 minutes: Cold blue palette, distorted audio. Explorable and manageable.
  • 2 minutes: Creepy whispers start.
  • 2.5 minutes: Howls and shrieks start.
  • 3 minutes: The Still Regard enters and begins hunting the player. It can follow the player back into the real world.

The Still Regard

A lizard creature covered in eyes. In the mirror world, its eyes are open and shoot beams — it takes more damage. In the real world, its eyes are closed — it takes less damage.

Its bestiary entry, written by the past character who encountered it once before: "Freaky lizard... thing (???)" accompanied by a bad sketch.

Guardian Eyes

Floating red eyes which can shoot beams, like the eyes on The Still Regard. They get closer and closer throughout the game, as if something had sent them.

Mirror World Diegetic Indicators

There is no timer shown. The mirror world communicates danger entirely through the world itself:

  • Shadow distortion: The character's shadow grows increasingly wrong the longer they stay, stretching at impossible angles, moving slightly out of sync. At two and a half minutes it begins moving independently of the player. It also tries to move away from The Still Regard, as if it is afraid.
  • Reflection lag: The player character's reflection in mirrors begins to lag slightly behind real movement as the timer progresses — a fraction of a second at first, but it increasingly slows down and changes appearance. By three minutes it is clearly wrong.

System 5: Stealth

The player can also cloak themselves using the mirror world. This makes them invisible to enemies in the normal world.

  • 0–30 seconds: Fully invisible
  • 30 seconds: Guardian eyes sense the player and surround them
  • 35 seconds: Guardian eyes alert nearby enemies
  • 45 seconds: Guardian eyes begin shooting beams, dealing damage

When the player surfaces, the guardian eyes disperse back into them, a visual reflection of the player anchoring both worlds together.

Stealth Diegetic Indicators

No stealth timer is shown on screen. The player reads their own character:

  • Cloak edges: The cloak becomes faintly visible at the edges as the stealth timer runs down — the cloaking effect degrading in real time. At 30 seconds the edges shimmer slightly. At 45 seconds the whole cloak is visibly unstable.
  • Sword glow: The sword suppresses its own glow during stealth — dims right down to avoid giving the player away. It brightens again the moment they surface. A player who forgets they are cloaked will notice their sword has gone quiet.

System 6: Mirror World Puzzles

Many puzzles require the mirror world, and they are in places like temples. The temple gets unlocked in the real world, but in the mirror world it is locked. The player can hear The Still Regard trying to get in, but failing, so the player can stay in the mirror world for as long as they want.

The mirror world has certain differences which can add some difficulty to puzzles:

  • Gravity distortion — Objects fall sideways, up, and bounce.
  • Mechanism desync — Moving platforms and doors run on different timing cycles than the ones in the real world.

3.4 Interactivity

Action and Feedback

Every on-beat hit produces distinct audio and visual feedback. Missing the beat has a clear response. Elemental activations are visually distinct. Harmony attacks are screen-filling events. Every player action is communicated clearly and immediately.

Short-Term Cognition

The rhythm combat system demands constant short-term attention: which instrument is prominent? Is a harmony approaching? Mirror world navigation adds parallel pressure: how long have I been in here? Can I solve this puzzle before the three-minute threshold?

Long-Term Cognition

Lore entries accumulate across the full game. The bestiary builds over time. Book entries grow more desperate in ways that only make sense in retrospect. Players who pay attention to the music and world are rewarded with recognition at the climax.

Emotional

The book's voice — past-you addressing future-you — is the emotional spine of the game. The opening cutscene shows a deliberate act whose meaning the player cannot understand yet. The mirror world's wrongness escalates incrementally throughout the entire game.

Cultural

The game's music draws influence from and collaboration with independent musicians whose work shaped the audio direction. These contributions are credited in full.

3.5 User Experience Design

Usability

There are barely any traditional menus, which keeps immersion. The tutorial is skippable, in case it is someone's second playthrough, or they are speedrunning the game.

There are barely any inputs. It is always disorienting when you play a new game and it has about 200 actions, so you don't know what key to press, or when. You can also rebind your actions to different keys, in case you are used to different controls. Timing is the only thing that matters.

UI

By default, there is no HUD whilst playing. However, for accessibility, you can toggle it to easier see what is happening in the game. The book is the main menu for everything, and is bound to escape.

Onboarding and Learning Curve

The game teaches through consequence rather than instruction. The rhythm system is introduced in a low stakes area with slower, simpler enemies before anything dangerous happens.

Scalable Difficulty

The difficulty is not the traditional number format. There is no reason why, I just wanted to be different. It goes: Relaxed, Easy, Medium, Hard, Extreme.

The difficulty can be changed at any time, and the default difficulty is Medium. These are the things the difficulty affects:

  • Beat Window — Relaxed is 300ms, Easy is 250ms, Medium is 200ms, Hard is 150ms, and Extreme is 75ms. The smaller the window, the less time you have to hit to the beat of the music. (These are temporary, I don't know what actually feels fast/slow)
  • Mirror World Timer — How long before the bad stuff happens (eyes, Still Regard appearing). Extreme is 2 mins until It appears, and Relaxed is 5 mins until It appears.
  • Enemy Aggression — How frequently and decisively enemies engage.

Accessibility

All accessibility options are toggleable individually through one of the book's pages.

  • Text size — book text and UI text scalable independently.
  • Subtitles — Vocal tone interactions display emotional intent and power in text (e.g. [threatening], [friendly]) for players who cannot distinguish pitch differences.
  • Reduced motion — harmonies flashing suddenly, and mirror world distortion can be reduced without affecting gameplay.
  • HUD — To easier see what is happening in the game.

Flow State

The rhythm system is structurally designed to produce flow. The music sets the pace — the player does not control the tempo, only their responses to it. The five-hit elemental build creates a short feedback loop inside a longer combat loop: each hit is satisfying, the activation is more satisfying, and the harmony — unpredictable and natural — is the peak. The difficulty sliders maintain flow across skill levels without changing the game's atmosphere or narrative.
