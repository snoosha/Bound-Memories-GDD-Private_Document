Section 01

High Level Concept Design

1.1 Working Title

The game is titled Bound Memories.

The title carries two meanings simultaneously. 'Bound' refers to the player character's situation — they are physically bound, sealed underground in a prison they locked themselves into — and to their obligation, tied to a past they cannot escape. 'Memories' is the entire substance of the game: chasing them, uncovering them, and deciding whether they are worth the cost of keeping. The title communicates what the game is about in two words.

1.2 Concept Statement

"An amnesiac necromancer wakes inside their own prison, retraces a past they deliberately erased, and must confront the consequence of what they once did. Explore, uncover, and choose whether remembering is worth the cost."

1.3 Genre

Bound Memories combines three genres, each reinforcing the others rather than competing with them.

Puzzle-Adventure (Primary)

The game is exploration-driven. The player moves through a large underground fortress called the Citadel, discovering areas, unlocking paths, and solving environmental puzzles. The puzzles are concentrated in the mirror world — a parallel dimension accessible through reflective surfaces — where physics behaves differently from the real world.

Rhythm-Combat (Secondary)

Combat is built around the music. Each instrument in the soundtrack has an associated element. Hitting enemies on the beat in sync with a specific instrument builds toward activating that instrument's elemental power. The player must listen actively and respond to what is currently playing.

Dark Fantasy Narrative

The story is a mystery told through environment, a physical book the player carries, and two endings neither of which is clearly correct. The tone is dark but not gratuitous — threat comes from atmosphere and absence rather than explicit content.

1.4 Target Audience

Primary Audience

  • Ages 13 to 25
  • Players who engage deeply with indie games — particularly Undertale, Hollow Knight, and Celeste — games known for strong atmosphere and emotional storytelling
  • Players motivated by mystery and lore, who enjoy piecing together a story from fragments
  • Players who enjoy music as a gameplay element, similar to rhythm games or Crypt of the NecroDancer
  • Players who appreciate dark aesthetics without gratuitous content

Secondary Audience

  • Players who enjoy completionist side content — optional rat quests, secret figurine battles, a full bestiary
  • Fans of specific online content creator communities whose characters appear as Easter egg figurines (explained in Section 3 — these references are purely cosmetic and the game is fully enjoyable without recognising them)

ESRB Rating Justification

Rated T (Teen). Fantasy violence (combat, necromancy, enemy death), dark thematic content (imprisonment, guilt, memory erasure, a character who did something terrible and chose to forget it), and mild horror elements (a parallel dimension populated by floating eyes and a pursuing creature). No sexual content, no real-world violence, no explicit language.

1.5 Unique Selling Points

Combat Built Around Active Listening

Most action games use music as atmosphere. In Bound Memories, the music is a direct gameplay input. Each instrument has an associated element. Five consecutive on-beat hits timed to a specific instrument activates that element's attack effect. The player must identify what is currently playing and respond. Special 'harmony' windows open when multiple instruments play simultaneously, producing massive combined attacks. This rewards listening rather than reflexes alone.

A Parallel World With Its Own Physics

The mirror world — accessible through reflective surfaces — is the same layout rendered cold blue and deep purple, with distorted gravity, intact ruins of destroyed structures, and mechanisms running on different cycles. It grows more dangerous the longer the player stays. After three minutes, a hostile creature enters and can follow the player back into the real world.

A Diegetic UI With Narrative Function

No traditional menus exist. Every interface element lives inside a physical book the player character carries. The book was written by the player character before losing their memories, giving instructions and warnings to their future amnesiac self. Its entries grow more desperate the deeper the game goes. The UI and the story are the same object.

A Mystery Told Through Environment

The opening shows the player character deliberately sealing themselves underground. The game never fully explains what they did — the player assembles the truth through lore fragments, environmental detail, and the tone of messages their past self left behind.

A Universal Vocal Power System

Every character communicates through short musical tones playing alongside text (similar to Undertale). Lower pitch indicates greater power — this is consistent across every entity in the world. The player character has the lowest tone of anything in the game. This lands differently once the story reveals what they are.

Section 02

Product Design

2.1 Player Experience and Game POV

Who is the player character?

The player controls an amnesiac necromancer — a magic user with the power to raise the dead and access a parallel dimension. They wake with no memory of who they are. A book sits in front of them with their name on the cover, written in their own handwriting. Everything the player learns about their character's identity comes through this book and the world around them.

The character is always cloaked. Their face is never visible. Their name is written on the book cover but always positioned just out of frame. Before losing their memories, the character left one rule in the book: NEVER show your face under ANY circumstances. The player follows this rule without knowing why. The ending reveals why.

What is the setting?

The game takes place entirely underground. The primary location is the Citadel — a vast gothic fortress built into a cave ceiling, with towers disappearing into stalactites and walls blending into cave rock. It is run by a crime lord referred to as the Mafia Boss, whose symbol — an eye motif — is carved into every surface.

The Citadel is populated by evolved humans (people who have lived underground long enough to grow small white feathered wings from their temples), goblins, and wandering undead. They run shops, trade, and exist here as their normal world.

What fantasy does the game grant?

The fantasy of being the most powerful entity in a room while being the least certain of who you are. The player character can raise the dead, manipulate a parallel dimension, and unleash elemental attacks — and cannot remember their own name. The game is about the tension between power and identity, and whether recovering your identity is worth what you find when you get there.

Target Emotions

  • Creeping dread — the mirror world grows more hostile over time; the eyes in every reflection get progressively closer throughout the entire game without the player being told this is happening
  • Curiosity — the mystery of what the player character did; the desperate tone of book entries; lore that never fully explains itself
  • Competence — mastering the rhythm system; learning the mirror world's rules; reading the boss fight through the music
  • Melancholy — the ending, regardless of which choice is made
  • Occasional comedy — certain characters and moments are deliberately funny, providing tonal relief without breaking atmosphere

2.2 Visual and Audio Style

Visual Style

2D pixel art from an isometric perspective (a top-down angled view giving depth without 3D geometry). Depth sorting is handled by assigning depth values based on vertical position so characters and objects layer correctly.

The real world uses warm dim cave tones — browns, dark greens, oranges. The mirror world renders the same layout in cold blue and deep purple. This contrast is one of the most important visual tools in the game. Combat harmony attacks produce massive multicoloured beam effects across the screen — especially striking against the dim cave palette.

Audio Style

Strings are the backbone. Violin and cello form the emotional core of exploration music. Additional instruments — electric guitar, bass, harp, and others — are introduced gradually. The music grows more distorted and frantic as the game progresses, reaching near-unrecognisable distortion before the boss arena.

The boss fight uses an original breakcore track built as a modular loop system — individual chunks sequence semi-randomly so the track never repeats identically across a long fight. Every instrument cue is timestamped, allowing GameMaker to trigger environmental hazards in sync with the music regardless of which loop is playing.

Vocal Texture System

Every character communicates through short musical tones playing alongside text boxes — similar to Undertale. Each character type has a distinct tone giving them vocal identity without voice acting. Lower pitch indicates greater power: this rule is universal and consistent across every entity. The player character has the lowest tone of any entity in the game.

2.3 Game World Fiction

The world is entirely underground — no sky, no surface, no outside. The Citadel dominates: a vast gothic fortress built into a cave ceiling, its towers disappearing into stalactites, its walls indistinguishable from cave rock. The Mafia Boss's eye symbol is carved everywhere.

Behind every reflective surface is the mirror world — the same space, cold and distorted, where physics behaves incorrectly and ruins destroyed in reality still exist intact. The player is the only being who can cross into it.

The player character wakes with no memory. The opening cutscene shows them walking deliberately into a sealed chamber and locking it from the inside. They did this to themselves. The game is the process of finding out why.

2.4 Monetization

Free to play. Optional cosmetic purchases include: colour palettes, book covers (changing the appearance of the diegetic UI object), and vocal textures (changing the player character's text sound). No purchases provide any gameplay advantage. A player who never spends money experiences the complete game identically to one who buys every cosmetic.

  • Visual Style: 2D isometric pixel art

Section 03

Detailed & Game Systems Design

3.1 Core Loops

Primary Combat Loop

Encounter enemy → listen to music → identify active instrument → build 5 on-beat hits to that instrument → activate elemental effect → watch for harmony windows → use necromancy on fallen enemies → move on. The loop rewards active listening at all times.

Exploration Loop

Move through Citadel → find mirror world access → enter mirror world → solve gravity-distortion puzzle or find lore → exit before timer reaches danger threshold → unlock new area → accumulate book entries → deepen understanding of central mystery.

Progression Loop

Combat and exploration earn stat points. Points are spent in the book across five stats. Better stats open new tactical options without closing off old ones.

3.2 Objectives and Progression

Opening and Tutorial

The player wakes with no memory. A book with their name on the cover contains a tutorial written by their past self. The player can follow it or skip it — skipping is thematically valid since the character has no memory anyway.

Short-Term Goals (First Hour)

  • Learn basic movement and combat
  • Understand the rhythm combat system through early encounters
  • Discover and enter the mirror world for the first time
  • Emerge from a tunnel into the full reveal of the Citadel — one of the game's most visually striking moments

Medium-Term Goals (Hours 1–3)

  • Explore the Citadel's multiple wings and areas
  • Navigate the Mafia Boss debt storyline
  • Find and complete optional rat side quests
  • Discover and decide what to do with the mysterious pink crystal
  • Accumulate lore entries revealing the player character's past

Long-Term Goals (Hours 3–5+)

  • Reach the boss arena
  • Defeat the boss fight boss
  • Receive the return of full memory
  • Make the ending choice

System 1: The Book (Diegetic UI)

No traditional menus exist. Everything lives inside a physical book the player character carries. It was written by their past self, for their future amnesiac self.

  • Cover — player character's name, always just out of frame, never readable
  • Save slots — written as journal entries dated in the player's handwriting
  • Stats page — five stats recorded as numbers, upgradeable with earned points
  • Bestiary — a page per creature. Sketch quality reflects encounter conditions: calm creatures have detailed sketches; rare or dangerous creatures have rushed, bad sketches. The final boss has no entry from past-you — after defeating it, the current player character writes their own.
  • Lore entries — unlock as the player explores. Always in first person, always in the player's handwriting. Tone shifts from calm and instructional early on to fragmented and desperate later.
  • Mirror world pages — appear blank in the real world; text becomes visible only in the mirror world
  • Tutorial — optional, written by past-you as instructions for a future self who remembers nothing

System 2: Combat — The Rhythm System

Combat is real-time with free movement. The player attacks by hitting enemies on the beat of the music. Each instrument in the soundtrack has an associated element. Five consecutive on-beat hits timed to a specific instrument activates that element's attack effect. The player must identify the prominent instrument and choose which element to build toward.

There is also a special musical phrase — the lick (E G A G F E D) — which works identically to the metal pipe: hit every note of the phrase on beat for a massive damage burst, miss any and the opportunity is gone until it returns.

System 3: Harmony

When multiple instruments play simultaneously — as happens naturally at certain points in the music — a harmony window opens. The player has approximately one to two seconds to hit exactly on beat. If successful, a harmony attack triggers. Harmony cannot be forced or built toward; it depends entirely on the composition, making it feel like a genuine reward for listening.

  • 2 instruments playing: Small coloured beam attack
  • 3 instruments: Medium effect
  • 4+ instruments: A massive beam crosses the entire screen in both directions — a rainbow cross that affects everything on screen

Each instrument contributes its own colour to the harmony visual, so the beam's colour tells the player exactly what combination they activated.

Harmony is forbidden in the mirror world. Attempting it causes rocks to fall from the ceiling. The in-world reason: a full harmony in a thin-boundary location would be catastrophically destabilising.

Separate from the rhythm combat system. The player can raise defeated enemies as temporary allies at the cost of resurrection energy. System 5: The Mirror World

A parallel layer of the game world, accessible through reflective surfaces in certain areas. Same physical layout, rendered cold blue and deep purple with distorted audio and altered physics. Only the player character can cross into it.

Access Rules

  • Thin boundary areas — ancient sites, places of emotional weight. Mirror world is simply accessible.
  • Thick boundary areas — ordinary lived-in spaces such as the tavern. Mirror world cannot be reached. These places are genuinely safe.
  • Boss arena — dedicated portals that open and close based on the boss's energy output. The boss does not control these intentionally — it is a byproduct of its nature.

Timer and Escalation

  • 0–2 minutes: Cold blue palette, distorted audio. Explorable and manageable.
  • 2 minutes: Whispers begin — lore fragments audible to players who stay long enough to listen.
  • 3 minutes: The freaky lizard thing enters and begins hunting the player. It can follow the player back into the real world.

The Freaky Lizard Thing

A creature covered in eyes. In the mirror world, its eyes are open and shoot beams — the creature is vulnerable to damage. In the real world, its eyes are closed — the player deals significantly less damage. Optimal play: lure it through into the real world when needed for breathing room, return to the mirror world to damage it properly.

Its bestiary entry, written by past-you who encountered it once before: "Freaky lizard... thing (???)" accompanied by a bad sketch.

The Guardian Eyes

Floating red eyes with no visible body, present in the mirror world and visible behind the player's reflection in every mirror throughout the game. They appear far in the background in the first mirror the player finds. They get closer as the game progresses. By the final area before the boss, they press against the surface of the mirror from behind. The game never explains them. There is no bestiary entry. They have been getting closer the entire time.

The player can use the mirror world to cloak themselves, becoming invisible to enemies in the real world. The stealth system has a strict timer with escalating consequences:

  • 0–30 seconds: Cloaked and invisible — the safe window
  • 30 seconds: Guardian eyes sense the player and surround them
  • 35 seconds: Guardian eyes alert nearby enemies
  • 45 seconds: Guardian eyes begin shooting beams, dealing damage

Surfacing mid-stealth causes immediate detection — a cloaked figure shimmering into existence in the middle of a room is noticed regardless of position. Stealth is a tool for planned movement, not an escape option. When the player surfaces, the guardian eyes disperse back into them — a visual reflection of the player anchoring both worlds together during stealth.

System 7: Mirror World Puzzles

Puzzle rooms are self-contained and entered through the mirror world. Their doors are shut — the freaky lizard thing cannot enter. The player can hear it attempting to get in and eventually giving up.

The mirror world's distinct physical properties used in puzzles — all coexisting simultaneously:

  • Gravity distortion — objects fall sideways, float upward, or fall at wrong speeds. Puzzles require working with the distortion rather than against it.
  • Intact ruins — structures destroyed in reality still exist in the mirror world. Collapsed bridges are whole. Old paths function.
  • Wrong light — light sources exist where there are none in reality; shadows fall incorrectly. Some paths are only visible in mirror world light.
  • Mechanism desync — moving platforms and doors run on different timing cycles than their real-world counterparts.
  • Rhythm mechanic integration — the ice effect from combat can hold mirror-world floating objects in place, combining both systems.

Early puzzle areas introduce one or two properties at a time. Late game areas combine multiple properties simultaneously.

System 8: The Mafia Boss Storyline

The Mafia Boss controls the Citadel. He is a large, physically imposing figure — deliberately intimidating — who wears a necklace and chains bearing his own eye symbol. He runs the Citadel through manufactured debts and enforced loyalty.

Early in the game, his henchmen approach the player in the tavern and press money into their hands — a fabricated loan for something they claim the player did. This debt is entirely invented. When the henchmen demand the player remove their hood to confirm identity, the player cannot comply — the book's rule forbids it. The player's unusually low vocal tone has already told the henchmen what they wanted to know, but neither side acknowledges this. One henchman begins to say "are you—" and is immediately cut off by the other.

The player is taken to the Mafia Boss's headquarters: a medium-sized office with a plush red chair, a grand desk, and various objects that can be examined with an investigate prompt (right click cancels the examine animation at any time). The boss explains the debt terms. The player may attempt to refuse — it does not matter. The debt is assigned regardless.

Later, during a second tavern visit, the Mafia Boss's henchmen return to collect. Annoyed by Kytti's music, they attack her. Kytti immediately and completely destroys them without warning or visible effort. She resumes playing, unbothered. The player pickpockets the loan money back from the wreckage. The debt is cleared.

After this, the player may optionally give items to the henchmen. There is no reward. There is no acknowledgement. The option exists.

System 9: Kytti

Kytti is a large, round, cream-coloured cat who sits on a stool in the tavern and plays music. The tavern is one of the few genuinely safe spaces in the game — the mirror world cannot be reached there, enemies do not enter, and the atmosphere is warm and comfortable.

Kytti's bestiary entry, written by past-you: "Cool cat kitty cat (music cat)". No stats, no threat assessment, no notes on behaviour. Past-you simply wanted to document that Kytti exists.

The Mafia Boss's henchmen, annoyed by her music during their second visit to collect the debt, attack her. This is their mistake. Kytti destroys them immediately and without visible effort, then resumes playing. The player pickpockets the loan money back. Kytti does not acknowledge any of this.

If the player also attacks Kytti: the music shifts to a lower pitch and the player is immediately killed. No warning, no health bar, no introduction. Kytti resumes normal activity, unbothered. The henchmen already made this mistake. The player does not need to repeat it.

Why this happens (mechanical, not shown to player): Kytti is a natural one-entity harmony — all instruments simultaneously, perfectly in sync. Idle play is passive. Any attack briefly interrupts this, but Kytti's retaliation immediately reharmonises as a full-power attack before anyone can capitalise. Building five on-beat hits against her is impossible. The game never explains this.

Kytti's battle music is lower pitched than the idle music, following the world's established rule: lower pitch indicates greater power. Kytti in battle mode is at full power. The system is consistent.

System 10: The Mysterious Pink Crystal

Somewhere in the Citadel, the player finds a pink crystal on the ground. When picked up, its name displays in large, heavy, imposing font: …

The game never names it, never explains it, and never tells the player what it is for. The only relevant book entry reads: "Do not carry what does not belong to you into places of power."

  • Carried into the boss fight: merges with the boss on entry, activating additional attack phases, draining resurrection energy faster, and triggering new hazards. The fight becomes significantly harder.
  • Used on a figurine in a shop: initiates a secret pocket dimension battle for that figurine (see System 11).

System 11: Figurine Easter Eggs and Secret Battles

Two shops in the Citadel contain shelves of small figurines. The player can examine any figurine. There is no collection mechanic, no reward for examining them — they are references to characters from specific online content creator communities. These references are purely cosmetic and the game is fully enjoyable without recognising any of them.

If the player carries the pink crystal when touching a figurine, they are pulled into a secret pocket dimension battle with its own rules and music:

  • Eggchan (giant blue eyeball head figure) — Battle: single devastating divine attack. Always wins. No exceptions.
  • Wemmbu (purple blob with tiara) — Battle: orbital strikes with targeting indicators, then heavy mace slams. Hard but beatable.
  • Flamefrags (red fire figure) — Battle: fast, aggressive, reads player movement. Hard but beatable.
  • Spoke (dark silhouette with flowing cape) — Battle: exploits game mechanics deliberately — teleports, ignores collision. Mostly wins.
  • Parrot (figure with colourful wings) — Battle: chess. The game transitions to a chess match. The player loses.
  • Theobald (round yellow bird with sunglasses) — Battle: TNT minecart explosions. Medium difficulty — easier if the player uses the mirror world to see where the next explosion will land before it arrives.
  • Slushly (yellow and purple figure) — Battle: robs the player mid-fight, uses their own stats against them but not effectively. Medium difficulty.
⚠ Development Flag — Licensing The music used in Slushly's battle has a licensing restriction. This music must be disabled and replaced before the game is released publicly. This must be actioned before release.

System 12: Rat Side Quests

Seven optional rat battles are hidden across different areas of the Citadel. Defeating a rat transfers its elemental ability to the final boss fight — the boss cycles through the abilities of defeated rats, each tied to a specific instrument in the boss fight's music. Players who completed rat quests will recognise each ability pattern from having already fought it.

  • 🧊 Ice Rat — frozen cave areas
  • 🔥 Fire Rat — volcanic/hot areas
  • ✨ Aether Rat — ethereal areas near mirror world thin spots
  • 🏜️ Sand Rat — sandy/dusty areas
  • 🌿 Jungle Rat — overgrown ruins
  • 💀 Dungeon Rat — deepest dungeon area ("Pulverising a Dungeon Rat into a Fibrous Powder"). Defeated rats transfer an ability to the boss fight. The Dungeon Rat was destroyed too thoroughly. It contributes nothing. There is a brief gap in the boss's ability cycle where its contribution would have been.
  • 🌑 Void Rat — deepest darkest area, final one

All seven rat battle themes are licensed from Azali (credited in game, used with permission).

System 13: The Boss Fight

The boss fight takes place in a vast arena — the thinnest point in the world, where the boundary between realities is nearly nonexistent. The cave exploration music cuts out. The breakcore track begins.

The boss is a small lizard-type creature — not to be confused with the freaky lizard thing from the mirror world, which is a different creature entirely. The arena is enormous. The game treats this with complete seriousness and never comments on the size disparity.

Music-Driven Hazards

Specific synthesiser sounds in the breakcore track trigger environmental hazards: ground pulling the player toward the boss, walls closing, floor tiles becoming dangerous. Players who learn to associate sounds with hazards can anticipate and avoid them. New players will be caught off guard.

Portal System

The arena contains mirror world portals that open and close based on the boss's energy output — not as a deliberate tactic, but as a byproduct of its nature. When open: mirror world available, certain attacks counterable from inside, harmony impossible, timer toward danger runs. When closed: real world only, harmony windows available, full necromancy active.

Necromancy Tug-of-War

The player raises fallen enemies as allies. Energy depletes, control flips to the boss who raises everything back. The player fights to reclaim resurrection control. The cycle repeats with escalating intensity.

Rat Abilities

If the player defeated rat side quests, the boss cycles through those elemental abilities. Each is tied to a dominant instrument in the breakcore track — the music signals what is coming. Players who completed rat quests recognise the patterns.

After the Boss

The player character opens the book. The screen fades to black. When it returns, a new bestiary entry exists — written by the current player character, not by past-you. The handwriting is slightly different. It reads: "kinda powerful lil guy". Below it is a rough sketch.

System 14: The Endings

When the boss is defeated, the player character is immediately trapped. The act of winning proves exactly why they needed to be contained. Full memory returns. The player character — and through the book entries and lore, the player — understands everything: what they did, why they sealed themselves away, why the book carries the warnings it does.

The game never directly states what the player character did. The truth is assembled from fragments. The player is trusted to reach their own conclusions.

Ending A — Seal Yourself

The memory of what they did is unbearable. The player character chooses to seal themselves away again — not because they are forced to, but because they cannot live with knowing. They close their eyes. No speech. No dramatic moment. It simply ends.

3.4 Interactivity

Action and Feedback

Every on-beat hit produces distinct audio and visual feedback. Missing the beat has a clear response. Elemental activations are visually distinct. Harmony attacks are screen-filling events. The metal pipe and the lick — all-or-nothing moments — are the loudest, most satisfying sounds in the game when landed. Every player action is communicated clearly and immediately.

Short-Term Cognition

The rhythm combat system demands constant short-term attention: which instrument is prominent? Is a harmony approaching? Is the tenth drum hit coming? Is the metal pipe or lick about to arrive? Mirror world navigation adds parallel pressure: how long have I been in here? Can I solve this before the three-minute threshold?

Long-Term Cognition

Lore entries accumulate across the full game. The bestiary builds over time. Book entries grow more desperate in ways that only make sense in retrospect. Rat side quests teach elemental attack patterns that the boss fight then deploys — players who paid attention are rewarded with recognition at the climax.

Emotional

The book's voice — past-you addressing future-you — is the emotional spine of the game. The opening cutscene shows a deliberate act whose meaning the player cannot understand yet. The mirror world's wrongness escalates incrementally. The boss fight boss's entry, written by the current player character for the first time, signals that they are becoming someone again.

Cultural

The figurine Easter eggs reference specific online content creator communities that influenced the game's development. They are never explained in game. The rat battle music is licensed from Azali, a musician whose compositional style influenced the entire audio direction of the game.

3.5 User Experience Design

Usability

Bound Memories eliminates traditional menu friction entirely. The book is the UI — a physical object the character already carries. There are no separate menu screens to navigate. The tutorial is optional, written in first person by the player character's past self, and framed as instructions left for a future amnesiac. It never reads like a game tutorial; it reads like a desperate note. Players who skip it are not penalised — the rhythm system is legible enough to learn through play.

Controls are minimal by design. Movement, interact, enter mirror world, and attack are the core inputs. The rhythm combat system adds no new buttons — it recontextualises existing attacks through timing rather than adding complexity.

UI — The Diegetic Interface

There is no HUD during exploration or combat. Health, resurrection energy, and stealth timer are all communicated through environmental and audio cues rather than on-screen bars. The screen edge darkens as stealth danger escalates. The music grows more distorted as the mirror world timer approaches its limit. Players read the world, not a UI overlay.

The book opens on demand and contains everything traditionally held in menus: stats, bestiary, lore, save slots, and settings. This keeps the screen clean during play while keeping all information accessible and in-world.

Onboarding and Learning Curve

The game teaches through consequence rather than instruction. The rhythm system is introduced in a low-stakes area with slow, simple enemies before anything threatening appears. The player discovers the beat window through trial and error — missing a beat makes a distinct sound; landing five in a row triggers a visible elemental effect. No tooltip explains this. The system reveals itself.

The mirror world escalation timer teaches itself the same way. The first visit is safe and exploratory. The whispers at two minutes are atmospheric, not threatening. The freaky lizard thing arriving at three minutes — with no warning, no tutorial box — teaches the rule permanently. Players remember consequences, not instructions.

Feedback and Responsiveness

Every player action has an immediate, distinct response. On-beat hits produce a tight audio cue and a small visual flash on the enemy. Missing the beat produces a different sound — lower, duller. Five consecutive on-beat hits to a specific instrument triggers a visible elemental activation with a unique colour and screen effect per element. Harmony attacks fill the screen. The metal pipe and the lick — all-or-nothing mechanics — are the loudest, most screen-filling moments in the game when landed correctly.

The vocal tone system adds a further layer: every interaction with an NPC communicates their relative power through the pitch of their text sound before the player has processed any other information. Lower pitch means more powerful. This rule is never stated.

Scalable Difficulty

Difficulty in Bound Memories scales across four independent sliders rather than a single easy/medium/hard setting. This allows players to tune the experience to their specific friction points without changing the game's tone or narrative.

  • Beat Window — controls how precisely timed an on-beat hit must be. Wider window makes rhythm combat more forgiving without removing the system. Narrower window rewards expert timing with no mechanical change — the satisfaction of precision is its own reward.
  • Mirror World Timer — adjusts how long the player has before the freaky lizard thing appears. Shorter timer increases pressure and horror. Longer timer allows more thorough exploration of mirror world puzzles without time stress.
  • Enemy Aggression — controls how frequently and decisively enemies engage. Lower aggression gives more time to read the music and build elemental combos. Higher aggression forces faster decisions.
  • Instrument Indicator — an optional visual indicator that highlights which instrument is currently dominant in the music. Off by default — players are intended to listen. Turning it on provides a visual anchor for players who find the audio identification step difficult, without removing the rhythm system itself.
Difficulty settings are accessed through the book and can be changed at any time, including mid-run. There is no penalty for adjusting them. The game does not track or display which settings the player used.

Accessibility

  • Colourblind modes — elemental attack colours and mirror world palette adjusted for deuteranopia, protanopia, and tritanopia. Harmony beam colours are remapped to remain distinguishable.
  • Remappable controls — all inputs reassignable. No chord inputs required by default.
  • Text size — book text and UI text scalable independently.
  • Subtitles — all vocal tone interactions display the emotional intent of the sound in text (e.g. [threatening], [friendly]) for players who cannot distinguish pitch differences.
  • Reduced motion — harmony screen-wipe effects and mirror world distortion can be reduced in intensity without affecting gameplay.
  • Pause anywhere — the game can be paused at any point including during rhythm combat sequences.

Flow State

The rhythm system is structurally designed to produce flow. The music sets the pace — the player does not control the tempo, only their responses to it. This removes the cognitive overhead of self-pacing and places the player in a reactive state where attention narrows to the immediate moment. The five-hit elemental build creates a short feedback loop inside a longer combat loop: each hit is satisfying, the activation is more satisfying, and the harmony — unpredictable and unrepeatable — is the peak.

The difficulty sliders maintain flow across skill levels by keeping the challenge close to each individual player's ability. A player struggling with the beat window widens it just enough to stay engaged. A player who finds combat too easy tightens it. The game's atmosphere and narrative are identical at every difficulty setting — flow is preserved without compromising the experience.

Boss Fight Track Technical Notes

The boss fight music is built as a modular system. Individual chunks are exported as separate audio files: an intro, multiple interchangeable loop sections, a breakdown, and a drop. GameMaker sequences the loop sections semi-randomly each fight. Every synthesiser cue is timestamped within its chunk, allowing GameMaker to trigger environmental hazards at the correct musical moment regardless of which loop is playing. Three phases of the fight align with three structural sections of the track. The metal pipe cue and the lick appear at fixed timestamps within their respective chunks.
